# The Launch World Selection

**Type:** Founding Wound (2 of 3)
**When:** 2031-2032, post-Founding Event

## Overview

After Dr. Okafor proved that Lattice transit worked, the question became: which world
do we go to first? The process by which the first Lattice destination was chosen was
political, not scientific. The tensions it created persist centuries later.

## The Wound

Six hundred worlds were theoretically reachable. The scientific criteria for selecting
the first transit destination were clear: stability, habitability, proximity, resonance
compatibility. But the decision was not made by scientists.

Governments, institutions, and factions competed to influence the selection. The first
world settled would carry enormous symbolic and economic significance — the first
Chronicle entries, the first dynasties, the first governance structures. Everyone wanted
to shape that beginning.

The selection process was compromised by political pressure. The chosen world was not
the best scientific candidate. It was the best political compromise. This is known,
documented, and still debated.

## Player Impact

- The first settled world carries the legacy of this decision
- Players who research the selection process find evidence of the alternatives
- The "roads not taken" are a source of ongoing faction tension
- Some worlds that should have been settled first were delayed by decades

## Faction Response

- **Continuationists** accept the compromise as necessary pragmatism
- **Returnists** argue the selection proved the Lattice should never have been used
  for colonisation
- **The Ascendancy** claims the selection was irrelevant — the Lattice itself chose
  where humanity would go

## Design Principle

The launch world selection should illustrate that even in an age of abundance, human
politics persist. The wound is not tragic — it is frustrating. Players should feel
that better choices were possible and that the consequences of this compromise are
still playing out.
