## RHYME DOCKS — Felix (3 entries needed → +3)

◆ The Limerick — Comedy in Five Lines
Type: cultural_milestone | Year: ~1820 | Location: Ireland/England

Ages 5–7: A limerick is a funny poem with five lines and a bouncy rhythm. The first, second, and fifth lines rhyme, and the third and fourth rhyme with each other. They almost always make you laugh.
Ages 8–10: The limerick form became popular in the 1820s and was popularized by Edward Lear. Its strict meter (anapestic) and rhyme scheme (AABBA) create a built-in comedic rhythm — the punchline lands on the fifth line because the structure demands it. Form creates humor.

Adventure: Reenactment — children write limericks in the Limerick Lighthouse using a rhyme-matching tool.
Fun Fact: Edward Lear often ended his limericks with the same word as the first line. Modern limericks usually end with a surprise word — the form evolved.

◆ The Oral Poetry Tradition — Before Writing
Type: scientific_principle | Location: Global

Ages 5–7: Before anyone could write, people remembered poems by saying them out loud over and over. Rhythm and rhyme helped them remember thousands of lines. The oldest poems were never written — they were spoken for hundreds of years before anyone wrote them down.
Ages 8–10: Oral poets used formulas — repeated phrases like Homer's "rosy-fingered dawn" — as memory aids. These formulas aren't laziness; they're technology. The repetition anchors memory. Neuroscience confirms that rhythm and pattern dramatically improve recall.

Adventure: Guided Expedition — Felix teaches memory techniques using rhythm, and children memorize a short poem using oral tradition methods.
Fun Fact: The Iliad is over 15,000 lines long. It was memorized and performed orally for centuries before being written down.

◆ Rap and Hip-Hop as Modern Poetry
Type: cultural_milestone | Year: 1973 | Location: Bronx, New York, USA

Ages 5–7: Rap is poetry set to a beat. Rappers use rhyme, rhythm, wordplay, and storytelling — the same tools poets have used for thousands of years, just with new music.
Ages 8–10: Hip-hop emerged from the Bronx in the 1970s. Its verbal techniques (internal rhyme, multisyllabic rhyme, flow, metaphor) are among the most technically complex in any poetic tradition. MCs are the modern inheritors of the oral tradition that Homer and the griots practiced.

Adventure: Guided Expedition — Felix connects dockside rhythms to hip-hop beats, showing that poetry adapts to its era.
Fun Fact: The word "rap" originally meant "to talk" in African American English. "Rapping" meant having a conversation — the musical form kept the conversational DNA.

## LETTER FORGE — Amara (3 entries needed → +3)

◆ The Invention of the Printing Press
Type: invention | Year: 1440 | Location: Mainz, Germany
Historical Figures: Johannes Gutenberg

Ages 5–7: Before Gutenberg, every book was copied by hand — one at a time. His printing press used movable metal letters to print hundreds of copies quickly. Books went from rare treasures to everyday objects.
Ages 8–10: Gutenberg's press democratized literacy. Before it, a Bible cost roughly the same as a house. Within 50 years, millions of books existed. Gutenberg himself went bankrupt, but his invention changed civilization more than almost any other.

Adventure: Reenactment — children set movable type in the Letter Forge and print a short message.
Fun Fact: Gutenberg's original Bibles are among the most valuable books in the world — about 49 copies survive and they're worth millions each.
Cross-connection: Story Tree (narrative distribution), Nonfiction Fleet (information access)

◆ Emoji — The Return of Pictographic Writing
Type: invention | Year: 1999 | Location: Japan
Historical Figures: Shigetaka Kurita

Ages 5–7: Emoji are tiny pictures you send in messages. A Japanese artist created the first 176 emoji in 1999. Writing in pictures is actually 5,000 years old — emoji bring us full circle back to hieroglyphics.
Ages 8–10: Shigetaka Kurita designed the first emoji set for a Japanese mobile carrier. The word "emoji" comes from Japanese (e = picture, moji = character). Emoji demonstrate that humans naturally gravitate toward visual communication — pictographic writing systems keep being reinvented.

Adventure: Guided Expedition — Amara shows the connection between ancient pictographs and modern emoji in the Emoji Kiln.
Fun Fact: The "Face with Tears of Joy" emoji was the Oxford English Dictionary's 2015 Word of the Year — the first time a pictograph won.

◆ Undeciphered Scripts — Writing We Can't Read
Type: unsolved_mystery | Location: Global

Ages 5–7: Some ancient writing systems have never been decoded. We can see the symbols but nobody knows what they mean. Entire languages are waiting to be understood.
Ages 8–10: Linear A (Minoan), Proto-Elamite (Iran), the Indus Valley script, and Rongorongo (Easter Island) remain undeciphered. Each represents a civilization's complete knowledge system locked inside unreadable symbols. Deciphering even one could rewrite history.

Adventure: Artifact Hunt — children examine undeciphered scripts in the Dead Script Archive and attempt pattern analysis.
Fun Fact: The Voynich manuscript, written in an unknown alphabet and language, has defeated codebreakers for over 600 years — including WWII Enigma codebreakers.

## PUNCTUATION STATION — Rosie (4 entries needed → +4)

◆ Aristophanes of Byzantium — Inventor of Punctuation
Type: person | Year: -200 | Location: Alexandria, Egypt
Historical Figures: Aristophanes of Byzantium

Ages 5–7: A librarian in ancient Alexandria invented dots to help actors know when to breathe while reading scripts aloud. These dots became our periods, commas, and colons.
Ages 8–10: Before punctuation, texts were written in continuous streams of letters with no spaces, no capitals, and no marks. Aristophanes introduced a system of dots at different heights to mark pauses. The modern punctuation system descended from his innovation 2,200 years ago.

Adventure: Guided Expedition — Rosie shows unpunctuated text and children experience how impossible it is to read.
Fun Fact: Ancient Roman texts had no spaces between words. A sentence looked like: THEQUICKBROWNFOXJUMPSOVERTHELAZYDOG

◆ The Interrobang — The Punctuation Mark That Combines ? and !
Type: invention | Year: 1962 | Location: New York, USA
Historical Figures: Martin K. Speckter

Ages 5–7: What if you want to ask a question AND shout at the same time?! An advertising executive invented a special mark called the interrobang (‽) that combines ? and ! into one symbol.
Ages 8–10: Martin Speckter was frustrated that there was no single mark for rhetorical questions asked with excitement. He combined "interrogation" (question) with "bang" (printer's slang for exclamation). Despite initial enthusiasm, it never became standard — we still use ?! or !? instead.

Adventure: Artifact Hunt — children find the interrobang in the Interrobang Terminal and practice using it.
Fun Fact: The interrobang has its own Unicode character (‽) and was briefly available on some Remington typewriters in the 1960s.

◆ The Oxford Comma — The Most Debated Punctuation
Type: scientific_principle | Location: Global

Ages 5–7: Should you put a comma before "and" in a list? "I love cats, dogs, and fish" OR "I love cats, dogs and fish"? People have been arguing about this for over a hundred years!
Ages 8–10: The serial (Oxford) comma is used before the final conjunction in a list. Its absence can create ambiguity: "I love my parents, Batman and Superman" (are your parents Batman and Superman?). AP Style omits it; Chicago Style requires it. The debate is real and ongoing.

Adventure: Reenactment — children see sentence ambiguities caused by missing Oxford commas and decide which version is clearer.
Fun Fact: A court case in Maine was decided based on a missing Oxford comma — dairy drivers won $5 million in overtime pay because a law was ambiguously punctuated.

◆ Punctuation Around the World
Type: scientific_principle | Location: Global

Ages 5–7: Not every language uses the same punctuation. Spanish puts an upside-down question mark at the BEGINNING of questions (¿). Arabic reads right to left, so its comma faces the other way. Greek uses a semicolon (;) where we use a question mark.
Ages 8–10: Punctuation is culturally specific. Japanese uses 。instead of a period and 、instead of a comma. Thai traditionally uses no punctuation at all — context determines pauses. Ethiopian script uses :: for periods. Punctuation reveals cultural assumptions about how language works.

Adventure: Guided Expedition — Rosie shows punctuation from 10 different writing systems.
Fun Fact: In some styles of Chinese, there are separate comma types for enumerating items (、) and for pausing within sentences (，).

## DIARY LIGHTHOUSE — Nadia (4 entries needed → +4)

◆ Anne Frank's Diary
Type: person | Year: 1942 | Location: Amsterdam, Netherlands
Historical Figures: Anne Frank

Ages 5–7: A girl named Anne Frank wrote a diary while hiding from danger during World War II. She was 13 when she started writing. Her private words eventually became one of the most-read books in the world.
Ages 8–10: Anne Frank wrote in her diary (which she called "Kitty") for over two years while hiding in a secret annex. She was discovered and died in 1945. Her father, the only family survivor, published the diary in 1947. Private words, never intended for an audience, became a document of historical testimony.

Adventure: Remembrance Wall — Anne Frank's story displayed in the Anne Frank Attic with context about private writing becoming public witness.
Fun Fact: Anne revised her diary herself, hoping to publish it after the war as a book called "Het Achterhuis" (The Secret Annex).

◆ Samuel Pepys' Diary — Witnessing the Great Fire
Type: person | Year: 1666 | Location: London, England
Historical Figures: Samuel Pepys

Ages 5–7: Samuel Pepys kept a diary that describes what it was like to see London burn in the Great Fire of 1666. Because he wrote everything down, we know exactly what that terrifying week was like.
Ages 8–10: Pepys' diary is one of the most important primary sources in English history. He wrote in shorthand to keep it private. His first-person account of the Great Fire, the Great Plague, and daily Restoration London is irreplaceable historical evidence.

Adventure: Time Window — children read excerpts from Pepys' fire account and see the event through his eyes.
Fun Fact: Pepys buried his wine and a wheel of Parmesan cheese in his garden to save them from the fire.

◆ The Power of Journaling — Science of Writing Your Feelings
Type: scientific_principle | Year: 1986 | Location: USA
Historical Figures: James Pennebaker

Ages 5–7: Scientists discovered that writing about your feelings in a journal makes you feel better. It's not magic — writing helps your brain organize confusing emotions.
Ages 8–10: Psychologist James Pennebaker's research showed that expressive writing (20 minutes, 3-4 days) measurably improves physical health, immune function, and emotional wellbeing. The act of converting emotions into words creates cognitive structure around chaotic experiences.

Adventure: Guided Expedition — Nadia leads a journaling exercise where children write about something that confused or bothered them.
Fun Fact: Pennebaker's studies show that people who write about difficult experiences visit the doctor less frequently in the months afterward.
Cross-connection: Wellness Garden (emotional health), Body Atlas (mind-body connection)

◆ Diary vs. Memoir — Private vs. Public Writing
Type: scientific_principle | Location: Global

Ages 5–7: A diary is writing for yourself. Nobody else has to see it. A memoir is writing about yourself FOR other people to read. They're different because who you're writing for changes how you write.
Ages 8–10: Diaries are unperformed — written without audience awareness. Memoirs are crafted with a reader in mind. Anne Frank's diary became a memoir-like text because she revised it with publication in mind. Nadia's diary remains private because she writes only for herself. The distinction teaches audience awareness — who you write for shapes what you write.

Adventure: Guided Expedition — children write the same event as a diary entry (private) and a story (public), comparing how the writing changes.
Fun Fact: Nadia says: "A diary is a mirror. A memoir is a window. Both show you, but only one shows everyone else."

## TRANSLATION GARDEN — Farah (4 entries needed → +4)

◆ Endangered Languages Project
Type: event | Year: 2012 | Location: Global

Ages 5–7: There are about 7,000 languages in the world. A language disappears on average every two weeks. When it does, an entire way of seeing the world is lost forever.
Ages 8–10: Of the world's ~7,000 languages, about 40% are endangered (fewer than 1,000 speakers). The Endangered Languages Project documents these languages before they vanish. Each language contains unique concepts, ecological knowledge, and ways of categorizing reality that exist in no other language.

Adventure: Guided Expedition — children learn a word from an endangered language and add it to the Endangered Greenhouse.
Fun Fact: The Pirahã language of the Amazon has no number words — only "few" and "many." Its speakers think about quantity differently from any other humans.

◆ The Tower of Babel — Why Languages Differ
Type: cultural_milestone | Location: Global (myth/concept)

Ages 5–7: Many cultures have stories explaining why people speak different languages. In one story, people tried to build a tower to the sky. The tower fell and everyone started speaking differently. It's a story about why communication is hard — and important.
Ages 8–10: The Tower of Babel is a origin myth for linguistic diversity. Linguistically, the real reason languages diverge is geographical separation, cultural drift, and time. Proto-Indo-European, spoken ~6,000 years ago, evolved into Hindi, Greek, English, Russian, and dozens more. Languages are rivers — they flow from common sources and split along the way.

Adventure: Guided Expedition — Farah traces language families from common ancestors, showing how "hello" evolved differently in each branch.
Fun Fact: English and Hindi are related — both descended from Proto-Indo-European. "Mother" in Hindi is "mātā," in English "mother," in Latin "mater" — the family resemblance is audible.

◆ Machine Translation — Computers Learning Language
Type: invention | Year: 1954 | Location: USA/Global

Ages 5–7: Computers can now translate between languages almost instantly. But they sometimes make funny mistakes because language isn't just words — it's culture, feeling, and context.
Ages 8–10: The Georgetown-IBM experiment in 1954 translated 60 Russian sentences into English. Modern neural machine translation (Google Translate, DeepL) uses AI trained on billions of translated texts. It's remarkable but imperfect — nuance, idiom, and cultural context still challenge machines.

Adventure: Reenactment — children compare human and machine translations, finding where the machine fails.
Fun Fact: An early machine translation of "The spirit is willing but the flesh is weak" into Russian and back to English allegedly produced "The vodka is good but the meat is rotten."

◆ Code-Switching — Speaking Two Languages at Once
Type: scientific_principle | Location: Global

Ages 5–7: Some people who speak two languages mix them in the same sentence — like saying "Let's go to the tienda" (mixing English and Spanish). This isn't confusion — it's a skill only bilingual people have.
Ages 8–10: Code-switching (alternating between languages within a conversation) requires exceptional cognitive control — the speaker must manage two grammatical systems simultaneously. Research shows that bilingual children who code-switch perform better on attention and problem-solving tasks.

Adventure: Guided Expedition — Farah demonstrates code-switching between her eleven languages and children practice with two.
Fun Fact: Farah code-switches unconsciously. She often doesn't realize she's changed languages until someone looks confused.

## NONFICTION FLEET — Birch (3 entries needed → +3)

◆ The Dewey Decimal System
Type: invention | Year: 1876 | Location: USA
Historical Figures: Melvil Dewey

Ages 5–7: Melvil Dewey invented a way to organize every book in the world using numbers. All knowledge divided into 10 main groups, each with 10 sub-groups, each with 10 more. You can find any book using just three numbers.
Ages 8–10: The Dewey Decimal System categorizes all human knowledge into 10 classes (000-999). It's used in libraries worldwide. Its limitations reveal its creator's biases — Christianity gets its own section while all other religions share one. Organizing knowledge is never neutral.

Adventure: Guided Expedition — children sort books in the Dewey Dock into correct categories.
Fun Fact: Dewey was a controversial figure — brilliant organizer but difficult person. His system, like all systems, reflects the biases of its time.

◆ Peer Review — How Science Checks Itself
Type: scientific_principle | Year: 1665 | Location: London, England

Ages 5–7: Before a scientific discovery is accepted, other scientists check the work. This is called peer review. It's like having your homework checked by the smartest kids in class.
Ages 8–10: Peer review was formalized with the Royal Society's Philosophical Transactions in 1665. Before a paper is published, experts in the field evaluate its methods, evidence, and conclusions. It's imperfect (biases exist, mistakes slip through) but it's the best system we have for separating reliable knowledge from unsupported claims.

Adventure: Reenactment — children "peer review" each other's findings from Discovery Trail experiments.
Fun Fact: Captain Birch says: "I don't trust claims until they've been reviewed. That's not cynicism. That's care."
Cross-connection: Discovery Trail (scientific method), Debate Arena (evaluating evidence)

◆ Confirmation Bias — Seeing What You Want to See
Type: scientific_principle | Location: Global

Ages 5–7: Sometimes people only notice facts that agree with what they already believe. If you think it's going to rain, you notice every cloud but ignore the sunshine. This is a trick your brain plays on everyone.
Ages 8–10: Confirmation bias is the tendency to search for, interpret, and remember information that confirms pre-existing beliefs. It affects everyone — scientists, journalists, and children. Captain Birch teaches it because the Nonfiction Fleet's mission is accuracy, and bias is the greatest threat to accuracy.

Adventure: Guided Expedition — children experience confirmation bias in a controlled exercise and learn to recognize it.
Fun Fact: Even scientists fall for confirmation bias. That's why peer review and replication exist — the system is designed to catch what individual humans miss.

## MARKET SQUARE — Carmen (4 entries needed → +4)

◆ Supply and Demand — The Invisible Balance
Type: scientific_principle | Location: Global

Ages 5–7: When lots of people want something and there isn't much of it, the price goes up. When nobody wants it and there's plenty, the price goes down. This is supply and demand — the invisible balance of every market.
Ages 8–10: Supply and demand is the fundamental mechanism of market economics. At equilibrium, the quantity buyers want matches the quantity sellers offer. Carmen's stalls demonstrate this every day — mango prices change with the season because supply changes with the harvest.

Adventure: Reenactment — children run a market stall with changing supply and demand conditions.
Fun Fact: Carmen can predict mango prices a month in advance based on rainfall patterns. Weather and economics are connected.

◆ Haggling — The Art of Negotiation
Type: cultural_milestone | Location: Global

Ages 5–7: In many markets around the world, the price on the tag is just the starting point. Buyers and sellers talk back and forth until they agree on a fair price. This is called haggling and it's one of the oldest skills in the world.
Ages 8–10: Fixed pricing is a relatively modern invention (mid-1800s department stores). For most of human history, every transaction involved negotiation. Haggling teaches communication, persuasion, value assessment, and compromise — skills that transfer far beyond markets.

Adventure: Reenactment — children practice haggling with NPC merchants in the Market Square.
Fun Fact: Carmen says: "A fair price is one both people smile at. If only the seller smiles, the price is too high. If only the buyer smiles, the price is too low."

◆ The Invention of Receipt
Type: invention | Year: -3200 | Location: Mesopotamia

Ages 5–7: The very first written documents weren't stories or poems — they were receipts. People wrote down "I gave you 5 sheep" on clay tablets so nobody would forget.
Ages 8–10: Writing may have been invented FOR commerce. The earliest Sumerian tablets are accounting records — inventories, receipts, debts. Literature came later. The mundane need to track transactions literally created literacy.

Adventure: Guided Expedition — Carmen shows ancient receipt tablets and children create their own transaction records.
Fun Fact: This means every story, every poem, every novel in human history may owe its existence to someone who needed to keep track of their sheep.
Cross-connection: Letter Forge (origins of writing), Tax Office (record-keeping)

◆ Fair Trade — Ethics in the Market
Type: cultural_milestone | Year: 1988 | Location: Global

Ages 5–7: Fair trade means making sure the people who grow food or make products get paid fairly. When you see a "Fair Trade" label, it means the farmer got a good price for their work.
Ages 8–10: The Fair Trade movement began with the Max Havelaar label in 1988. It certifies that producers in developing countries receive a minimum price, workers have safe conditions, and environmental standards are met. It demonstrates that markets can be structured to prioritize fairness, not just profit.

Adventure: Guided Expedition — Carmen explains fair trade principles using her own three-generation market as an example.

## SAVINGS VAULT — Abernathy (4 entries needed → +4)

◆ The Rule of 72
Type: scientific_principle | Location: Global

Ages 5–7: A magic math trick: divide 72 by the interest rate, and you get how many years it takes your savings to double. At 6%, your money doubles in 12 years.
Ages 8–10: The Rule of 72 is a mental shortcut for estimating compound interest doubling time. It works because ln(2) ≈ 0.693, and for rates between 2-15%, dividing 72 produces a close approximation. Mr. Abernathy calls it "the most important number in finance."

Adventure: Guided Expedition — children apply the Rule of 72 to different interest rates and watch Savings Trees grow.
Fun Fact: At 1% interest, doubling takes 72 years. At 12%, only 6 years. The difference between 1% and 12% is the difference between slow and transformative.

◆ The Piggy Bank — A History of Personal Saving
Type: invention | Year: ~1400 | Location: Europe

Ages 5–7: The piggy bank got its name from an accident! "Pygg" was a type of clay used to make jars where people kept coins. Over time, "pygg jar" became "piggy bank" and potters started making them shaped like pigs.
Ages 8–10: The etymology of "piggy bank" illustrates linguistic evolution. In Middle English, "pygg" was an orange clay used for household containers. By the 18th century, the connection between "pygg" and "pig" had been lost, so potters created pig-shaped banks. The misnaming stuck because it was charming.

Adventure: Reenactment — children design their own saving container and set savings goals.
Fun Fact: Mr. Abernathy keeps a simple clay jar (not pig-shaped) on his desk. "Saving doesn't need to be fancy. It needs to be consistent."

◆ Inflation — Why Money Shrinks Over Time
Type: scientific_principle | Location: Global

Ages 5–7: The same coin buys less today than it did when your grandparents were young. Prices slowly go up over time. This is called inflation, and it means saving money isn't enough — your savings need to GROW just to keep up.
Ages 8–10: Inflation erodes purchasing power. A dollar in 1970 had the buying power of about $8 today. If savings don't grow at least as fast as inflation, you're effectively losing money by saving it. This is why savings accounts pay interest — and why the interest rate matters.

Adventure: Guided Expedition — children see how the same coin buys less over time in a historical price simulation.
Fun Fact: In 1900, a loaf of bread cost about 5 cents in the US. Today it costs about $3. That's inflation over 125 years.

◆ Emergency Fund — Saving for the Unknown
Type: scientific_principle | Location: Global

Ages 5–7: Sometimes unexpected things happen — a bike breaks, shoes wear out, a pet gets sick. Having money saved for emergencies means you're ready for surprises.
Ages 8–10: Financial advisors recommend 3-6 months of expenses in an accessible emergency fund. Mr. Abernathy learned this the hardest way possible — his bank collapsed and his savings were inaccessible. His Emergency Fund Bunker exists because preparation is the best protection against the unpredictable.

Adventure: Guided Expedition — children build an emergency fund in the Savings Vault, then face simulated unexpected expenses.
Fun Fact: A Federal Reserve study found that 40% of American adults cannot cover a $400 emergency expense. Abernathy considers this the most important financial statistic anyone can learn.

## BARTER DOCKS — Tomás (4 entries needed → +4)

◆ The Invention of Paper Money
Type: invention | Year: 618 | Location: China (Tang Dynasty)

Ages 5–7: Carrying metal coins was heavy. Chinese merchants started using paper notes instead — lighter, easier to carry. Paper money was born.
Ages 8–10: China invented paper money during the Tang Dynasty (~7th century), centuries before Europe. Merchants deposited coins with trusted agents and received paper receipts. The receipts became tradeable. The concept is identical to modern banking: paper represents value stored elsewhere.

Adventure: Guided Expedition — Tomás shows how paper money evolved from receipts, and children create their own representative currency.
Fun Fact: Marco Polo was astonished by paper money when he visited China. Europeans didn't adopt it for another 400 years.

◆ Cryptocurrency — Digital Money
Type: invention | Year: 2009 | Location: Global
Historical Figures: Satoshi Nakamoto (pseudonym)

Ages 5–7: Some money now exists only on computers — you can't hold it or see it, but people agree it has value. It works the same way as the Yap stone money: everyone agrees, even though nothing physical moves.
Ages 8–10: Bitcoin was created in 2009 by the pseudonymous Satoshi Nakamoto. It uses blockchain technology — a distributed ledger where everyone holds a copy. Like Yap stone money, ownership is recorded by consensus, not by physical possession. The connection between ancient and modern is striking.

Adventure: Guided Expedition — Tomás draws the parallel between stone money, paper money, and digital money.
Fun Fact: Nobody knows who Satoshi Nakamoto is. The creator of a currency worth hundreds of billions of dollars is anonymous.

◆ Hyperinflation — When Money Loses Meaning
Type: event | Year: 1923 | Location: Weimar Germany

Ages 5–7: In Germany after World War I, money lost value so fast that people needed wheelbarrows full of cash to buy bread. Children used stacks of bills as building blocks because the paper was worthless.
Ages 8–10: At its peak, Weimar Germany's inflation reached 29,500% per month. Prices doubled every 3.7 days. Workers were paid twice daily and spent immediately before prices rose again. It proves that money's value depends entirely on trust — and trust can collapse.

Adventure: Reenactment — children experience accelerating prices in a virtual market, seeing firsthand how inflation destroys purchasing power.
Fun Fact: A loaf of bread cost 250 marks in January 1923 and 200 billion marks by November 1923.

◆ The Gold Standard — Money Backed by Metal
Type: scientific_principle | Year: 1821 | Location: United Kingdom/Global

Ages 5–7: Long ago, paper money was a promise — you could trade it for gold at a bank. This was called the "gold standard." Most countries stopped doing this, but the idea that money should be "backed by something" is still debated.
Ages 8–10: The gold standard tied currency value to a fixed weight of gold. Britain adopted it formally in 1821. The US abandoned it in 1971 under Nixon. Modern "fiat" currency has value because governments say it does and people trust them. The shift from backed to fiat currency is one of the most important monetary transitions in history.
